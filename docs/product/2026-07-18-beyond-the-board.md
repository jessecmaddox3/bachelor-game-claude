# Beyond the Board: Product Direction

> **TL;DR:** The bachelor board is the first format, not the whole product. The larger idea is a system of specific, well-designed invitations that help people escape boredom, try unfamiliar things, contribute to a group, and create stories together. Keep the board builder excellent and stable, then build a challenge library that can power kids' afternoons, family weekends, friend trips, personal experiments, and recurring community circles.

## Recommendation

Preserve `/gameboard` as the dependable names-plus-activities-to-PNG/PDF tool. Build the broader idea beside it as a challenge system, not inside the poster renderer. The same structured challenge library can eventually feed a printable board, a phone-friendly session list, a card deck, a personal challenge season, or a recurring group competition.

The core promise should be:

> Give me a reason to do something worth remembering.

The product is not a chore chart, a dare generator, or a habit tracker. It creates designed permission to try things that would otherwise feel too random, small, awkward, or easy to postpone. A library alone is not enough. The meaningful loop is commitment, light tracking, accountability, reflection, and memory.

## The Human Idea

The cabin weekend worked because the board turned idle time into shared possibility. It gave thirty people a common language, permission to be playful, and a reason to notice each other's actions. The points mattered, but the real output was stories.

That mechanism travels well. A child can earn points for reading a book in character voices or testing three paper airplane designs. A family can build a Saturday around unfamiliar food, neighborly help, and a ridiculous photo. An adult can spend a month learning to like olives. A group that meets regularly can use challenges as the shared commitment that makes it easier to become "our people" without requiring a religious or ideological center.

The smallest unit is a challenge. The larger unit is belonging through action.

## Product Principles

1. **Specific beats virtuous.** "Do something nice" is vague and forgettable. "Write a three-sentence note naming something you admire about a person" is doable and meaningful.
2. **A little stretch beats maximum difficulty.** The goal is expansion, not punishment. A good challenge is one step outside the participant's normal pattern.
3. **Stories beat streaks.** Optimize for moments people retell, artifacts they keep, and skills they unexpectedly discover.
4. **Contribution belongs beside novelty.** Cleaning the cabin kitchen, teaching a child a game, and making coffee for the group can be as worthy as the wild activity.
5. **Competition should reveal possibility.** Points add energy and structure, but the system should not reward recklessness, wealth, athletic advantage, or public embarrassment.
6. **Consent is part of completion.** No challenge is complete if it depends on pressuring, filming, touching, pranking, or exposing someone without clear permission.
7. **The audience changes the catalog.** Kids, families, adult friends, coworkers, and solo participants need separate eligibility rules, not one list with a weak content filter.
8. **Completion should be legible without surveillance.** Self-attestation, a witness, a small artifact, or a reflection is enough. The product should not require constant tracking.

## What Makes a Challenge Good

Every published challenge should pass this test:

- **Concrete:** A person can tell exactly what to do.
- **Bounded:** It has a natural finish line.
- **Right-sized:** The expected time and effort are clear.
- **Slightly surprising:** It interrupts an ordinary pattern.
- **Story-producing:** Completion creates a memory, artifact, skill, contribution, or connection.
- **Accessible:** It offers an adaptation when ability, age, money, location, or equipment could exclude someone.
- **Safe and consensual:** It does not reward harm, humiliation, dangerous consumption, illegality, or nonconsensual interaction.
- **Scorable:** Points reflect the intended kind of stretch, not an invitation to escalate risk.

## The Challenge Model

The library should be structured across a few dimensions so the same content can be reused intelligently:

- **Audience:** kids, teens, family, adults, solo, friends, couples, community
- **Setting:** home, cabin, neighborhood, school-safe, outdoors, travel, anywhere
- **Duration:** five minutes, under an hour, half-day, day, week, month
- **Stretch:** gentle, curious, bold, wild
- **Mode:** solo, pair, team, whole group, service to group
- **Theme:** make, move, taste, learn, connect, contribute, explore, perform, notice, persist
- **Requirements:** materials, cost, mobility, weather, transportation, supervision
- **Completion:** self-check, witness, artifact, photo optional, reflection
- **Safety:** age floor, adult supervision, allergy note, physical caution, alcohol-free default, medical exclusion

This structure lets a parent ask for "ten indoor challenges for ages six to ten with no shopping," while a cabin organizer can ask for "twenty adult group challenges, half competitive and half cooperative, with nothing that requires driving."

## Scoring Without Making It Weird

Use a simple base scale:

- **1 point, Spark:** quick, welcoming, low-friction participation
- **2 points, Try:** some effort, novelty, or contribution
- **3 points, Stretch:** real discomfort, skill, persistence, or social courage
- **5 points, Quest:** a multi-step or multi-day commitment with a meaningful finish

Points should be assigned by expected stretch within the selected audience. Reading aloud may be one point in an adult cabin pack and three points for a shy eight-year-old's personal season. The product should never add points for greater alcohol volume, physical danger, humiliation, injury, vomiting, deprivation, or involving an unwilling stranger.

For recurring play, do not crown only the person with the largest raw total. A monthly **Expansion Cup** can recognize several forms of growth:

- Most Curious
- Most Courageous
- Most Generous
- Most Persistent
- Best Story
- Best Teammate

That resolves the instinct Jesse described as "the person who most expanded" without pretending personal expansion is one objective number.

## Formats the Library Can Power

The poster remains valuable when a group shares a physical place and time. Other situations need different containers:

- **Game Board:** named people, rows of activities, points, print-ready PNG/PDF
- **Session List:** a phone-friendly menu for an afternoon, party, cabin, or rainy day
- **Challenge Cards:** printable or physical cards drawn at random
- **Personal Quest:** one chosen stretch with a duration, check-ins, and a final reflection
- **Season:** a four-week mix of small sparks and larger quests
- **Circle:** a recurring group where people choose, complete, and tell the story of a challenge
- **Family Board:** cooperative points, age-aware alternatives, and shared rewards
- **Community Cup:** teams earn across curiosity, contribution, craft, movement, and connection
- **Story Archive:** completed challenges preserved as a short note, artifact, or optional photo

## Product Shape

The system has four layers:

1. **Library:** safe, tagged, specific challenges and curated packs.
2. **Composer:** chooses an audience, setting, time window, tone, and constraints.
3. **Format:** board, list, cards, season, or circle.
4. **Story:** lightweight completion and reflection that gives the activity meaning after the points are counted.

The existing board builder is the first format layer. It should consume selected challenges, but it should not own the taxonomy, safety logic, or long-term participation model.

The bachelor board already handles commitment and memory unusually well. Printing a large object makes the game feel real, and the marked-up board becomes a keepsake. The broader platform still needs the middle of the loop: a lightweight way to mark completion, see the group's progress, and answer one reflection question after a meaningful challenge. A QR code on a printed board could eventually open a link-based scoreboard that requires no account. That would connect the physical artifact to the live experience without turning the product into a social network.

## Initial Curated Packs

The first public version should favor a few excellent packs over a giant undifferentiated feed:

1. **Cabin Weekend:** the original use case, social energy, games, contribution, and memorable low-logistics challenges.
2. **Kids' Rainy Day:** indoor, low-cost, age-aware creative and movement activities.
3. **Family Saturday:** cooperative challenges that produce connection rather than sibling scorekeeping alone.
4. **Try Something New:** approachable adult horizon-expansion with food, books, skills, places, and conversation.
5. **Personal Experiment:** one-week and one-month challenges inspired by Void Loves Challenges.
6. **Good Neighbor:** specific acts of contribution and local connection without vague moral language.
7. **Friends at Home:** high-energy social play that does not depend on alcohol.
8. **Make and Perform:** art, invention, storytelling, music, and low-stakes public courage.

Adult alcohol content, if it exists, should be a clearly separate opt-in 21+ pack. It should reward tasting, knowledge, hosting, and moderation, never speed or volume. Extended fasting and medically meaningful physical challenges should not appear in the general generator. They can be discussed as personal stories, but publication requires explicit health review and exclusions.

## MVP on jessemaddox.com

The immediate public architecture should stay simple:

- `/gameboard`: the stable board builder and export tool
- `/challenges`: a later landing page that explains the idea and lets someone browse or generate a small pack
- A shared challenge catalog in source control, with validation and curated pack membership
- Four launch filters: who, where, how long, and how much stretch
- Four launch outputs: copy list, print sheet, board import, and shareable link

Do not add accounts, a social feed, public leaderboards, AI-generated dares, or automated photo judging to the first version. Those features add moderation and privacy burdens before the core challenge quality has been proven. A no-account, link-based completion tracker is the one digital loop worth testing early because it directly answers whether people actually follow through.

## Human Review Questions

Before building `/challenges`, decide together:

1. Is **Void Loves Challenges** the public brand, the founder story, or the name of the personal-experiment mode?
2. Should the first broader experience lead with kids/families, adult personal expansion, or group weekends?
3. Does the default scoring celebrate individual totals, cooperative group totals, category awards, or a mix?
4. How much adult wildness belongs in the core brand versus an explicitly separate pack?
5. Is the recurring community idea a near-term product mode or a later real-world program supported by the tool?

## Recommended Next Build Sequence

1. Finish the board's human-reviewed visual fidelity and make names plus activities reliably produce PNG and PDF.
2. Establish the typed challenge catalog and validation rules.
3. Curate and human-test the first four packs: Cabin Weekend, Kids' Rainy Day, Family Saturday, and Try Something New.
4. Build a small `/challenges` browser and pack generator without accounts.
5. Add a no-account completion link and one reflection prompt, then connect it to printed output with a QR code.
6. Test one real family session, one adult group session, and one personal week.
7. Use those stories to decide whether the next format should be cards, seasons, or circles.

This broader system now has enough continuity and scope to deserve its own project after human review. The board builder can remain the production renderer, while the challenge platform gets a separate name, roadmap, and repository boundary once the public identity is chosen.
