# Starter Challenge Catalog

> **TL;DR:** This is a human-review catalog of 100 specific challenge ideas across kids, families, adult groups, personal experiments, contribution, and controlled wildness. The language is intentionally concrete enough to become product data later. Points use 1 Spark, 2 Try, 3 Stretch, and 5 Quest. All photo or video evidence is optional.

## How to Review This Catalog

The catalog is arranged as ten possible packs with ten challenges each. It is not yet the production database. Human review should mark each item **keep**, **revise**, **move**, or **cut**, then choose the first four packs to encode and test.

Every challenge needs a clear finish line and a story, skill, artifact, contribution, or connection. Kid challenges assume an adult chooses activities appropriate to the child's age and abilities. Anything involving food requires allergy awareness. Anything involving tools, water, heat, roads, or unfamiliar places requires appropriate adult judgment and supervision.

## Pack 1: Kids Make Something

This pack turns an ordinary room and basic supplies into visible accomplishments. Most challenges work for ages six and up with easy adaptations.

1. **Three-Plane Test** (2): Fold three different paper airplane designs, name them, and measure which flies farthest.
2. **Book-Weight Bridge** (3): Build a bridge from paper, tape, or blocks that can hold one book for ten seconds.
3. **Tiny Museum** (2): Choose five objects, make a label for each, and give someone a two-minute museum tour.
4. **Invent an Animal** (2): Draw a new animal, name it, and explain three adaptations that help it live somewhere specific.
5. **Cardboard Upgrade** (3): Turn one clean box into something useful, wearable, playable, or decorative.
6. **One-Song Instrument** (2): Make an instrument from household materials and use it for one whole song.
7. **Best Fort Feature** (2): Build a fort with one special feature, such as a mail slot, secret entrance, sign, or reading light.
8. **Marble Run Rescue** (3): Build a track that keeps a marble or small ball moving for at least five seconds.
9. **Snack Architect** (2): Arrange a snack as a face, animal, landscape, or building before eating it.
10. **Family Flag** (2): Design a flag with three symbols and explain what each represents.

## Pack 2: Kids Read, Notice, and Learn

These are more memorable than "read a book" because each adds performance, curiosity, or an artifact.

11. **Seuss in Three Voices** (2): Read one Dr. Seuss book aloud using at least three character voices.
12. **Ending Detective** (2): Pause a new story before the ending, write or draw a prediction, then compare it with what happens.
13. **Word Collector** (2): Find five unfamiliar words in a book, guess their meanings, and check the guesses.
14. **Teach the Grown-Up** (2): Learn one fact well enough to teach it to an adult without reading from the source.
15. **Living-Room Librarian** (1): Sort ten books into categories of your own invention and explain the system.
16. **Map the Story** (2): Draw a map showing where the important events in a story happened.
17. **Fact or Foolery** (2): Tell the group two true facts and one invented fact about a topic, then see who spots the invention.
18. **Question Safari** (2): Write five questions about something nearby, then find real answers to at least three.
19. **Old Thing Interview** (3): Ask an older person about an object they remember from childhood and draw or describe it.
20. **Book Trailer** (3): Give a one-minute, spoiler-free performance that makes someone want to read a finished book.

## Pack 3: Kids Move and Explore

The goal is playful movement and observation, not athletic ranking. Every challenge can be adapted for mobility and space.

21. **Animal Movement Circuit** (1): Cross a room four ways, each inspired by a different animal.
22. **Ten-Minute Trail Map** (2): Walk a safe route and draw a map with five landmarks afterward.
23. **Backyard Field Guide** (2): Find and sketch five different leaves, rocks, insects, clouds, or signs of animals.
24. **Playground Game Teacher** (2): Teach an adult or another child a playground game and complete one round together.
25. **Sock-Ball Course** (2): Design a five-station indoor course and complete it with a rolled-up pair of socks.
26. **Shadow Tracker** (3): Mark or photograph one object's shadow three times in a day and explain what changed.
27. **Color Hunt** (1): Find one safe object for every color of the rainbow without moving anything that should stay put.
28. **Quietest Minute** (1): Sit outdoors silently for one minute, then name or draw five sounds.
29. **Family Stretch Leader** (1): Lead everyone through five gentle stretches, with permission to skip or adapt any move.
30. **Personal Best, Your Way** (2): Choose one repeatable movement, set a safe baseline, practice, and improve it once.

## Pack 4: Family Saturday

These challenges create cooperation and stories across ages. Scoring should usually be shared by the whole family.

31. **New Park Vote** (2): Visit a park no one in the family has used, then let each person nominate its best feature.
32. **Five-Dollar Feast** (3): Give each child a small shared budget, choose one meal addition together, and help prepare it.
33. **Reverse Teacher** (2): Each child teaches an adult one skill for ten minutes.
34. **No-Recipe Dessert** (3): Invent a simple dessert from ingredients already available, then name it as a family.
35. **Neighborhood Bingo** (2): Make a nine-square observation card and complete one safe walk together.
36. **Family Time Capsule** (3): Add one note or drawing from each person, choose an opening date, and seal the container.
37. **Grandparent Hotline** (2): Call an older relative or friend and ask the same three questions about childhood.
38. **Living-Room Premiere** (3): Make and perform a three-minute show where every family member has a role.
39. **One-Hour Yes Day** (3): Let the children design one hour from pre-approved, free choices, then follow their plan.
40. **Kindness Relay** (2): Each person completes one specific helpful act for another household member before dinner.

## Pack 5: Cabin Weekend

This is the direct descendant of the original board. It mixes play, contribution, performance, and low-logistics competition without making alcohol the engine.

41. **First-Up Coffee Crew** (2): The first person awake prepares coffee, tea, or breakfast setup for the group.
42. **Signature Cabin Dish** (3): Cook one quality dish for at least four people and get two honest tasting notes.
43. **Paper Airplane Distance Line** (2): Build one plane on site and cross a clearly measured distance line.
44. **One-Take Karaoke** (2): Perform a complete song selected by someone else, solo or with a recruited partner.
45. **Teach Your Obscure Game** (2): Teach a game that at least half the group has never played and finish one round.
46. **Ten-Person Toast** (2): Give a toast that names something true and specific about the group or guest of honor.
47. **Cabin Improvement** (2): Notice and complete one useful task before anyone assigns it to you.
48. **Mystery Ingredient Cook-Off** (3): Use one agreed ingredient in a snack or dish, with taste judging but no elimination bracket.
49. **Ridiculous Portrait Studio** (2): Direct a consensual themed group portrait using only items already at the cabin.
50. **Midnight Story Circle** (3): Get at least six people together and prompt everyone to tell a true two-minute story.

## Pack 6: Friends at Home

These work for an ordinary evening and do not require a party venue, purchases, or alcohol.

51. **PowerPointless Talk** (2): Give a three-minute presentation on a topic you know unusually well, with no slides required.
52. **Blind Snack Ranking** (2): Taste three safe, common foods without seeing the labels and rank them with tasting notes.
53. **Theme-Song Entrance** (1): Choose a song for another person and stage their ten-second entrance with consent.
54. **One-Minute Documentary** (3): Make a sixty-second documentary about an ordinary object in the room.
55. **Bad Art Gallery** (2): Everyone draws the same subject in five minutes, then the group gives each work a sincere award.
56. **Recreate the Photo** (3): Recreate an old group photo or a public-domain artwork using what is available.
57. **Secret Skill Lesson** (2): Teach the group a small skill most people did not know you had.
58. **Three-Ingredient Challenge** (2): Make a snack using exactly three agreed ingredients, with allergy-safe substitutions allowed.
59. **Dub the Scene** (3): Mute a short, appropriate video clip and perform new dialogue live.
60. **Compliment With Evidence** (1): Tell someone one thing you value about them and include a specific example.

## Pack 7: Try Something New

This adult horizon-expansion pack favors approachable novelty. It should feel inviting, not like self-improvement homework.

61. **Unknown Shelf** (2): Read at least fifty pages from a genre you normally avoid.
62. **New Cuisine, One Dish** (2): Order or cook one dish from a cuisine you have never knowingly tried.
63. **Beginner Class** (3): Attend one introductory class in a subject where you are a true beginner.
64. **Transit Tourist** (3): Use public transit to visit one local place you have never entered.
65. **Ask for the Recommendation** (2): At a bookstore, market, or restaurant, try one specific recommendation from a worker.
66. **Museum Wild Card** (3): Visit a small museum or gallery you would normally pass and choose one object to remember.
67. **Learn the Chorus** (2): Learn and perform the chorus of a song in a language you do not speak fluently.
68. **Cook the Intimidating Recipe** (3): Make one recipe you have avoided because of a single unfamiliar technique.
69. **Local Event Roulette** (3): Attend one public event selected from a pre-screened list of things you would not normally choose.
70. **One Real Conversation** (2): Ask a willing acquaintance one question that goes beyond small talk and listen without redirecting to yourself.

## Pack 8: Personal Experiments

These are descendants of Void Loves Challenges. The point is to test a curiosity and notice what changes, not to prove toughness.

71. **Taste Acclimation** (5): Choose one safe food you dislike, try a small serving in five different preparations over a month, and record the result.
72. **Seven-Day Genre Swap** (5): Replace your normal entertainment genre with a different one for a week and finish one full work.
73. **Phone-Free First Hour** (5): Keep the first waking hour screen-free for seven days and note what fills the space.
74. **Vegetarian Week** (5): Eat vegetarian for seven days, plan for nutrition and allergies, and cook at least two unfamiliar meals.
75. **Daily Tiny Drawing** (5): Make one five-minute drawing every day for fourteen days and keep them together.
76. **The Early Version** (5): Wake thirty minutes earlier for seven days and use the time for one chosen purpose.
77. **Compliment Practice** (5): Give one specific, sincere compliment each day for ten days without commenting on appearance.
78. **One Drawer a Day** (5): Empty, decide, and reset one drawer or small storage area daily for a week.
79. **Local Curiosity Month** (5): Visit four nearby places you have never entered, one per week, and write three sentences about each.
80. **Beginner's Twenty Minutes** (5): Practice one unfamiliar skill for twenty minutes on ten separate days.

## Pack 9: Good Neighbor and Contribution

These replace vague kindness prompts with visible, bounded acts. People receiving help should retain dignity and the ability to decline.

81. **Named Thank-You Note** (2): Write a short note naming one specific thing a person did and why it mattered.
82. **Two-Bag Walk** (2): Fill two small bags with litter on a safe route while using gloves and local disposal rules.
83. **Porch Help Offer** (2): Offer one specific, low-pressure household task to a neighbor who can comfortably decline.
84. **Free Little Library Upgrade** (2): Add one suitable book you own to a nearby free library and straighten the shelf.
85. **New-Parent Drop-Off** (3): Arrange a useful food or supply drop-off at a time chosen by the recipient, with no expectation to host.
86. **Local Review With Detail** (2): Write a truthful review naming a specific strength of a small local business or community service.
87. **Skill Donation Hour** (3): Give one hour of a useful skill to a person or organization that has asked for it.
88. **Introduce Two People** (2): With both people's consent, connect two people who share a useful interest or need.
89. **Public-Space Reset** (2): Leave a shared room, park table, campsite, or play area noticeably better than you found it.
90. **The Useful List** (3): Create and share a concise local resource list for a real need someone has named.

## Pack 10: Bold and Wild, With Guardrails

Wild should mean vivid, socially brave, unusual, or committed. It should not mean dangerous. These need explicit opt-in and an easy no-penalty alternative.

91. **Open-Mic Three Minutes** (5): Perform at a legitimate open mic with material prepared in advance.
92. **Formalwear Errand** (2): Complete one ordinary public errand in formal clothes with no disruption or deception.
93. **Sunrise Mission** (3): Reach a legal, safe viewpoint before sunrise with a buddy and a transportation plan.
94. **Cold-Water Dip, Supervised** (3): Complete a brief cold-water dip only at a designated location with a buddy, safe conditions, and no medical contraindication.
95. **Twenty-Four-Hour Creation** (5): Announce a small creative project, finish it within a day, and share the result with one trusted person.
96. **Dinner From a Hat** (3): Randomly select from three pre-screened unfamiliar restaurants or cuisines and follow the draw.
97. **Public Beginner** (3): Try a visible beginner activity where mistakes are expected, such as a dance lesson, pottery wheel, or climbing orientation.
98. **Backyard Campout** (3): Spend a full night outside at home or an approved campsite with suitable weather and equipment.
99. **Ask the Expert** (3): Contact a willing expert or practitioner and ask for a fifteen-minute beginner conversation.
100. **The Courageous Invitation** (5): Invite a specific person to do a specific meaningful activity you have hesitated to propose, with a graceful way to decline.

## Explicit Exclusions From the General Generator

The historical bachelor board included activities that rewarded drinking volume, vomiting, bleeding, restraint, sleep deprivation, and similar outcomes. Those can remain part of the preserved historical artifact, but they should not seed a general-purpose challenge system.

The generator should reject or quarantine:

- injury, illness, vomiting, or property damage as completion conditions
- rapid or high-volume alcohol consumption
- extended fasting or medically meaningful deprivation
- humiliation, nudity, sexual conduct, or recording without explicit consent
- pranks or interactions that make an unwilling person part of the challenge
- trespassing, evasion, theft, deception, or illegal activity
- untrained use of weapons, fire, deep water, heights, vehicles, or dangerous tools
- challenges where spending more money creates a scoring advantage
- public leaderboards for children

## Strong Candidates for the First Human Test

Start with a short, balanced set rather than the most spectacular items: Three-Plane Test, Seuss in Three Voices, Tiny Museum, Family Time Capsule, Reverse Teacher, First-Up Coffee Crew, Cabin Improvement, One-Take Karaoke, Unknown Shelf, New Cuisine One Dish, Taste Acclimation, Named Thank-You Note, Skill Donation Hour, Sunrise Mission, and Twenty-Four-Hour Creation.

That test set covers kids, family, group, personal, contribution, and boldness while exposing the important product questions around points, proof, adaptation, and storytelling.
