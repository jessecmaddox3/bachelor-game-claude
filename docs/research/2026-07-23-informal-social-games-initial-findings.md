# Informal Social Games: Initial Findings

> **TL;DR:** The first research pass found 25 promising game families and a useful vocabulary for finding more. The best umbrella phrase is **informally transmitted, low-equipment social games**. The strongest next step is parallel research across folklore archives, camp and scouting sources, road-trip traditions, pub and campus games, and concealed-rule or always-on games.

## What We Found

There is no single established name for the whole category. Useful research terms include:

- Folk games, traditional games, playlore, childlore, and children's folklore
- Pervasive games, ambient games, ongoing games, background games, and always-on games
- Games with concealed rules, rule-discovery games, and observation games
- No-prop games, no-equipment games, camp games, road-trip games, pub games, and drinking-rule games
- Call-out games, spotting games, attention games, social elimination games, and selection rituals

Searching descriptions is essential. A title such as "My Team" is too broad to retrieve reliably, while a query such as "game where you claim strangers for your zombie apocalypse team" describes the actual mechanic.

Community forums are valuable for discovery and variant names, but weakly sourced rules should remain provisional until triangulated.

## Research and Catalog Standards

For the full catalog:

- Treat a game family and its variants separately. Do not manufacture a single "official" rule from conflicting traditions.
- Require two independent sources for a verified entry. Three independent community attestations can qualify an orally transmitted game when no institutional source exists.
- Mark one-source discoveries as provisional.
- Record the historical rule separately from a safer or non-drinking adaptation.
- Exclude coercive, humiliating, illegal, or meaningfully dangerous mechanics from the playable catalog. Keep notable exclusions in a research ledger so later agents do not repeatedly rediscover them.
- Deduplicate by mechanic, not title. Compare temporal mode, trigger, prohibition or goal, detector, response, outcome, and equipment.

## Product Connection

These games reinforce the case for an optional **Activity Guide** after the one-page scorecard. A board can use a short label such as "Buffalo Someone," while page 2 can explain the full rule, variants, equipment, age or alcohol context, and a non-drinking adaptation. General weekend rules and activity-specific instructions should remain separate controls.
